06 / Finance analysis

What portfolio optimisation found in seven ETFs

I used daily adjusted prices to test how a long-only portfolio would have behaved from January 2018 to March 2025. The result is useful, but it is also very dependent on that period.

Main result

The highest in-sample Sharpe ratio came from a concentrated mix of QQQ and gold.

That is a model output, not an investment recommendation.

01

The question

How much could diversification reduce risk across US shares, international shares, bonds, property and gold? I compared seven liquid ETFs and solved for the lowest volatility portfolio, the highest Sharpe ratio, and the efficient frontier between them.

The original notebook was lost. I rebuilt the analysis from the surviving export using the same tickers and date range. The figures and calculator now share one return series and one covariance matrix.

What drove the result

QQQ had the strongest annualised return in the sample. Bonds had the lowest volatility. Gold combined a relatively strong return with low correlation to shares.

06

Where I would be careful

The model only knows this one sample. It rewards assets that performed well between 2018 and early 2025, which helps explain the heavy QQQ and gold allocation. A different start date, risk-free rate or return estimate would produce different weights.

There is no out-of-sample test, rebalancing rule, tax treatment or transaction cost assumption here. I would use this to inspect trade-offs. I would not copy the weights.

Method note

How the page is calculated

Adjusted closing prices come from Yahoo Finance through yfinance. Returns and covariance are annualised over 252 trading days. SLSQP solves the long-only portfolios with weights constrained to sum to 100%.
